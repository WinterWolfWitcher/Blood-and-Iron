Introduction

The State of Things

Whether this land sits somewhere on a world you might recognize, or somewhere else entirely, is a question nobody has thought to ask. There are more immediate concerns. There are always more immediate concerns.

Seven kingdoms occupy this continent. They have been here long enough to have stopped asking where they came from. They trade, they war, they intermarry their nobility and then dispute the inheritance two generations later. They have roads, churches, markets, and standing armies. They have a shared faith that has done its best to explain the world and has mostly succeeded — until recently.

The wilderness between settlements is older than any kingdom. What lives in it has never been fully catalogued. What was once dismissed as the superstition of village elders and the embellishment of old songs is becoming, with some urgency, a matter of military and theological concern.

Two kingdoms — Dravek and Kelnvar — are eight months deep into the most active engagement of a war that has been grinding for years. The prize is Strelnov, a silver-rich border region that funds armies, which means whoever holds it wins and whoever wins holds it. Currently Kelnvar holds the advantage. Dravek is pushing to reclaim lost ground.

During a Dravek offensive in the highlands northeast of Strelnov, something descended from the sky. Eyewitness reports describe a creature of enormous size. It did not distinguish between sides. It burned what it did not take. It took a horse and departed.

The reports are being taken seriously. This is new.

The Incident

What Came Down From the Greymarch

"It came from the northeast. We heard it before we saw it — not a sound any of us had a name for. It passed over the engagement without slowing. It did not distinguish between our colors and theirs. The fire was not directional. It burned what was beneath it and kept moving. It took one of the supply horses — took it whole, in its claws — and rose back into the cloud cover. We counted eleven dead. We did not know what to write in the report. We wrote what we saw." — Field Report, Dravek Forward Position, Greymarch Approach

Every kingdom has stories about dragons. The Church has, for two centuries, classified such stories as folk superstition — the kind of fearful imagination that thrives in isolated villages and fades under the light of proper education and faith. Murals in older churches still depict them, though the newer clergy prefer not to explain what they are.

There are now forty-three eyewitnesses to the Greymarch incident. The reports are consistent. The Church has not yet issued a formal response. It is understood that one is being prepared.

The more pressing question — the one that soldiers are asking quietly in their camps, that village priests are being asked and cannot answer — is not whether dragons are real. That question has been answered. The question now is what else is.
